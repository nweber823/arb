1. Introduction to Arbitrage Trading

Definition and Principles

Arbitrage trading is a strategy that exploits price differences of identical or similar financial instruments across different markets or forms. The fundamental principle of arbitrage is to simultaneously purchase and sell an asset to profit from a difference in price. In theory, these price differences should not exist in perfectly efficient markets, but in reality, market inefficiencies create opportunities for arbitrage.

Key principles of arbitrage include:

  • Risk Neutrality: Pure arbitrage involves no risk when executed perfectly, as it exploits definite price discrepancies.
  • Market Efficiency: Arbitrage opportunities exist due to temporary market inefficiencies, which tend to disappear as traders exploit them.
  • Execution Speed: Successful arbitrage often depends on rapid identification and execution of trades before others can exploit the same opportunity.
  • Capital Efficiency: Many arbitrage strategies require significant capital to generate meaningful returns from small price discrepancies.
  • Technological Edge: Modern arbitrage relies heavily on sophisticated technology for data analysis and trade execution.

Types of Arbitrage Strategies

Our research identified several major types of arbitrage strategies:

  1. Statistical Arbitrage: Uses mathematical models to identify pricing inefficiencies based on historical statistical relationships between securities.
  2. Triangular Arbitrage: Exploits price discrepancies among three different currencies or assets by converting one to another, then to a third, and finally back to the original.
  3. Merger Arbitrage: Capitalizes on price discrepancies that occur during corporate mergers and acquisitions, typically by buying shares of the target company after a merger announcement.
  4. Spatial Arbitrage: Exploits price differences of identical assets across different geographical locations or exchanges.
  5. Convertible Arbitrage: Involves taking a long position in a convertible security (like a convertible bond) and a short position in the underlying common stock.
  6. Index Arbitrage: Exploits price differences between index futures contracts and the underlying basket of stocks that make up the index.

Evolution of Arbitrage Trading

Arbitrage trading has evolved significantly with technological advancements:

  • Traditional Arbitrage: Historically involved manual identification of price discrepancies across physical markets.
  • Electronic Arbitrage: Emerged with electronic trading platforms, enabling faster execution and broader market access.
  • Algorithmic Arbitrage: Current state where sophisticated algorithms automatically identify and execute arbitrage opportunities.
  • Statistical Arbitrage: Modern approach using advanced statistical methods to identify probabilistic arbitrage opportunities rather than risk-free ones.

As markets have become more efficient and algorithmic trading more prevalent, pure arbitrage opportunities have become rarer and shorter-lived. This has led to the development of more sophisticated approaches that rely on statistical relationships and incorporate multiple data sources.

4. Statistical Pairs Trading Core

The foundation of our statistical arbitrage strategy is the identification of cointegrated pairs of securities that exhibit stable statistical relationships.

Pair Selection Process

Methodology

  1. Correlation Analysis:
    • Calculate historical price correlations between stocks within the same sector
    • Filter pairs with correlation coefficients above a threshold (e.g., 0.7)
  2. Cointegration Testing:
    • Apply Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test to identify cointegrated pairs
    • Ensure p-value is below threshold (e.g., 0.05) to confirm cointegration
  3. Spread Stability Analysis:
    • Calculate the spread between paired securities
    • Analyze the stationarity and mean-reversion properties of the spread
    • Calculate half-life of mean reversion to filter pairs
  4. Fundamental Similarity Check:
    • Verify pairs have similar business models, market cap, and growth profiles
    • Ensure pairs are exposed to similar risk factors

Signal Generation

Once cointegrated pairs are identified, we generate trading signals based on deviations from their statistical relationship.

Methodology

  1. Z-Score Calculation:
    • Calculate the z-score of the spread between paired securities
    • Z-score = (Current Spread - Mean Spread) / Standard Deviation of Spread
  2. Entry Signals:
    • Generate long signal when z-score < -threshold (e.g., -2.0)
    • Generate short signal when z-score > threshold (e.g., 2.0)
  3. Exit Signals:
    • Generate exit signal when z-score returns to mean (e.g., 0.0)
    • Generate exit signal when z-score crosses opposite threshold
    • Generate exit signal after maximum holding period

7. Risk Management Framework

Position-Level Risk Management

Stop-Loss Mechanisms

  1. Statistical Stop-Loss:
    • Exit positions when z-score exceeds predefined thresholds
    • Typically set at 3-4 standard deviations from mean
  2. Absolute Stop-Loss:
    • Exit positions when loss exceeds predefined percentage of allocation
    • Typically set at 2-3% of portfolio value
  3. Volatility-Adjusted Stop-Loss:
    • Adjust stop-loss levels based on pair volatility
    • Wider stops for more volatile pairs

Portfolio-Level Risk Management

Diversification Rules

  1. Pair Diversification:
    • Limit exposure to any single pair (typically 5-10% of portfolio)
    • Maintain at least 5-10 active pairs when fully invested
  2. Sector Diversification:
    • Limit exposure to any single sector (typically 20-30% of portfolio)
    • Ensure representation across multiple sectors
  3. Strategy Diversification:
    • Allocate capital across statistical and event-driven components
    • Vary holding periods and signal types
